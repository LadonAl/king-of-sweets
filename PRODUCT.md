# Product

## Register

brand

## Platform

web

## Users

Primarily Newcastle locals and the nearby Syrian and Arab community deciding whether to visit the shop at 404 Westgate Road. Three secondary audiences matter: delivery-app browsers across Tyneside choosing a dessert shop from their sofa, people planning weddings and Eid or Ramadan gatherings who order large custom trays, and Arabic-speaking newcomers and international students looking for a taste of home. The site speaks to all of them in two languages — English at `/` and Arabic (full RTL) at `/ar/`.

## Product Purpose

The marketing site for King of Sweets (ملك الحلويات), a family-run Syrian sweets shop in Newcastle upon Tyne — kunafa, baqlawa and cheese sweets handmade daily. The site exists to turn curiosity into a shop visit: success is someone tapping "Get Directions" or walking in saying they found the shop online. Delivery-app orders are the fallback conversion for people who can't come in.

## Positioning

Family recipes carried from Damascus, made by hand since 2004. Heritage and the owner's personal story lead every screen — the craft came from Damascus and it's alive on Westgate Road.

## Conversion & proof

- Primary CTA: visit the shop (Get Directions). Secondary: order via the delivery-app links for visitors who can't make it in.
- The line a visitor remembers after 10 seconds: Damascus craft, made by hand in Newcastle since 2004.
- Belief ladder: (1) this is real Syrian craft — family recipes from Damascus, not a generic dessert shop; (2) it's made fresh by a real person whose story I've just seen; (3) people in Newcastle already love it — then the visit follows naturally.
- Proof on hand: three real Google reviews (Umair Sheikh, Mimi, Umar Wasim), already on the site in `src/data/site.ts`. More photos and reviews are coming later — drop new material into `.impeccable/assets/proof/` and it gets worked in.

## Brand Personality

Generous, proud, bustling. The voice of the souq stallholder who piles the tray high — abundance and energy, not whispered refinement. Scrolling the page should feel like the bustle of a Damascus market: appetite-driven, warm, alive. The "King" in the name is worn with a grin, not a velvet rope.

## Anti-references

- A takeaway listing site: a generic Just-Eat-style menu grid with prices — transactional, no story, no soul.
- A sterile luxury patisserie: French-minimal, white-marble, whisper-quiet — too precious for a family sweets house.

## Design Principles

- The counter, not the catalogue. Sell the experience of standing in front of piled-high trays, not a menu database. Every section earns its place by making a visit more likely.
- Abundance over restraint. Full trays, real photography, generous spacing and scale. When in doubt, show more sweets, never more whitespace.
- Heritage is a person, not a pattern. The Damascus story is told through the owner, his photos and his craft — never through decorative cultural wallpaper.
- Both languages are first-class. The Arabic page is a native experience — typography, rhythm and layout designed for RTL — never a mirrored afterthought.
- Proof from the neighbourhood. Real Google reviews, the real storefront, the real counter. Authentic and local beats polished and generic.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

WCAG 2.2 AA: body text at ≥4.5:1 contrast, full keyboard navigation, visible focus states, and `prefers-reduced-motion` alternatives for all animation. Bilingual delivery means both the LTR and RTL experiences must meet the same bar.
